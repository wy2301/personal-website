# What Hiring Managers Actually Look For on PM Personal Websites

**Source:** Sarah Chen — Senior Product Manager & Hiring Manager (12 years in PM, ~25 hiring searches)

---

## The Reality of How Hiring Managers Review Your Site

- Expect **60–90 seconds** of attention at most
- A polished design alone won't get you the interview — substance wins over style
- The bar for grabbing attention is high, so every element needs to earn its place

## What Sarah Wants to See

### Show the Hard Calls, Not the Happy Path

- Skip the generic "identified problem → shipped feature → metrics went up" arc
- Focus on **tradeoffs**: what did you say no to?
- Talk about what **broke** along the way and how you handled it
- Candidates who discuss underperformance and honest diagnosis stand out more than those with a perfect narrative

### Be Specific About YOUR Role

- "Led cross-functional teams" means nothing without context
- Show the actual mess: misalignment between engineering and design, leadership pushing a different direction, unclear data
- Make it clear what **you** personally decided, pushed back on, or navigated

### Depth Over Breadth

- Feature **2–3 projects max** and go deep
- Covering twelve surface-level projects signals an inability to prioritize — a red flag for a PM

## How to Structure Each Case Study

1. **Start with real tension** — not "the company needed to improve retention" but "we were losing 15% of users in the first week and nobody could agree on why"
2. **Describe what you specifically did** — the call you made, where you pushed back, what you decided with incomplete information
3. **Be honest about outcomes** — if it only partially worked, say so; explain what you'd do differently now
4. **Lead with the "so what"** — put what made the project hard or interesting up front; don't bury the insight

## Key Takeaways

- Judgment and decision-making under uncertainty matter more than frameworks
- Honest reflection ("here's what I'd do differently") signals maturity
- Don't make the reader work to find the interesting part — they might just click away
- Format doesn't matter much (long-form or structured with headers) as long as the insight is easy to find
